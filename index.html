Answers to Written Questions

Part 1

This dashboard explores the top video games, video game genres, and video game publishers according to this Kaggle dataset.
To answer the first question of what the top video game is, I created a dynamic bar chart that plots the top games according to global sales, first
for games released at any time (the all-time button), and then for specific decades (to see what has changed across the two most recent decades).
The map graph is used to answer the question of what the top genre is in each country. Using the broken-down sales in the dataset, I preprocessed
the data to break down total sales for each genre within a specific region. The top genre is showed as a tooltip upon hovering on the country.
To answer the third question of what the top publisher in each genre is, I preprocessed the dataset again externally to group a publisher's
global sales for their games by genre and add up total sales for all their games in that genre. Then I used this data to plot a scatter graph of
vs. global sales (total), and upon hovering each data point, you can see the top publisher for that genre and exactly how many games they have sold
within the genre (over all-time included in the dataset).

D3 was helpful because there was a lot of online documentation available and I could use additional packages like TOPOJSON to easily create decent
maps. I also liked that D3 is built on javascript, which means that it has a lot of options for customisability and interactivity for our plots.
Additionally, I also liked that there are pre-set plot types in D3, which made creating new visualisations (particularly as someone who had not done
this before) fairly intuitive. The fact that it can use JSON, CSV files (which are pretty common) also meant that I could easily preprocess my data externally.

D3 may not be the best visualisation tool for all purposes — if, for example, the creator is not particularly technical then perhaps a more visual editing interface
may be a lot more useful and easier to use. The fact that all shapes (from the rectangles to the circles) were all manually created means this was not so much
of a "quick" visualization tool but an in-depth, longer process. Additionally, there is no 'suggested charts' type, and the freedom that D3 gives you may
not be as appropriate if you are unsure of what type of chart/graph may suit your data best, as it is quite annoying to switch between chart types.

Part 2

I believe my dashboard's colours are quite accessible as I've designed it in order to keep it accessible according to the guidelines given in several ways. A big one for me
was using colours that would be distinguishable by the colour-blind, while still making it visually appealing to those who are not. To do this, I selected
multiple shades of muted red that would not be quite bright, but had different brightness levels so it could be told apart even when in greyscale (to ensure
this), I tried looking at my dashboard in greyscale. However, I believe there are a lot of other ways my dashboard's accessibility could be improved,
particularly in terms of digital accessibility — I have significant information in the tooltips of graphs 2 and 3 (the map and scatter plots), which means that users
who are unable to access these lose out on this information. If I had more time, I would alter it so that the map would be coloured differently depending on its
most popular genre (this would be particularly complex since I don't have much experience with TOPOJson). Additionally, I would also label the points on
the scatter plots with their top publisher, either through a colour and a legend or by text. I didn't do the text because I believe it would have
cluttered the visualisation. If I had more time, I would perhaps find more contrasting colours for the selectively-coloured scatter graph in order to make it
clearer to those without access to tooltips and were colour-blind. If I had more time (and knowledge of this!) at the time of creation I would also
possibly look at the Contrast Checker to make sure that my colours "mathematically" are contrasting enough, since using my own eye for greyscale might not be the best gauge.
One thing that I implemented after reading this was a legend and selective colouring, to make the scatter plot digitally more accessible to and to emphasize
the main point of the graph, which is the top publisher of the genre.
